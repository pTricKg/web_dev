The Maxwell House, landmarked in 2004, is believed to have been built in 1898, although some family narratives say the home was completed in 1905. The house retains some of its original architectural features, including cedar clapboard siding, a stacked flagstone foundation and cellar, original porch columns and interior doors. But the relatively simple cottage is more significant for its role in the social history of Lafayette. Original owner David Maxwell, then 23, and his wife Ellen Dick Maxwell, then 17, left Scotland and landed in Pennsylvania in April 1881. In 1884, they moved to Walsenburg and then, a year later, to a homestead near Erie. In May of 1890, the family moved to town and eventually built their home at 406 E. Baseline Road. A miner by trade, and an early organizer of unionization activities in local mines, David Maxwell was seriously injured in a workplace accident that ended his mining career and caused him to wear a shoe that was built up several inches. He was a member of the I.O.O.F. and the United Mine Workers of America. He was also a member of the Lafayette Volunteer Fire Department. One of Maxwells’ sons, John “Scotty” Maxwell and his wife, Kate McNeill Maxwell, lived in the home from 1930 to 1942. He was a member of the Lafayette Fire Department, a justice of the peace, a president of the United Mine Workers Local and a sergeant at Arms at a National Coalminers Union in Chicago.  Their other children were Ann Maxwell Coward, Julian Maxwell Irwin, Jean Maxwell Gaboury, David E. Maxwell, Cecil Maxwell and Gilbert Ronald Maxwell. David and Ellen Maxwell made Lafayette their home until their deaths, his in 1936 and hers in 1945. The home now is owned by Janet Richardson and her family.
